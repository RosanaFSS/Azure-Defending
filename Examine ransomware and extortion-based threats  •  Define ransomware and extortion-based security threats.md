```
▪   Ransomware is malware that encrypts files, alters access, or exfiltrates data to force payment.
▪   Ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) and cryptocurrencies scaled attacks by enabling less-technical actors.
▪   Paying a ransom offers no guarantee and can still lead to data loss, leaks, reputational damage, or harm to people.
```

```
▪   Ransomware payouts and incidents surged in 2021, costing organizations billions and hitting the U.S. and Europe hardest.
▪   Indirect targets include personal IoT devices and connected vehicles that can provide attacker footholds.
▪   Recovery choices are paying ransom (often incomplete and risky) or restoring systems—65% of data is typically returned and affected firms face high repeat-attack and failure rates.


Immediately verify and test immutable, offline backups and a documented incident response plan (roles, recovery time objectives, and regular backup validation) to avoid paying ransoms and shorten recovery time.
```


```
▪   Commodity ransomware: prebuilt kits sold for easy, opportunistic attacks.
▪   Human‑operated attacks target high‑value organizations for larger payouts.
▪   Ransom distributed denial-of-service attacks: occurs when legitimate users can't access their normal system because it has been flooded with spurious traffic from a cybercriminal.  Variations of the DDoS attack:  Smurf attack  & 
SYN flood
▪   Encryption and locking malware attacks: Encryption ransomware attacks are where the cybercriminal adds extra code to files to prevent them being accessed on the users' IT system; this is known as encryption. The ransomware can be downloaded onto a system via several different routes. For example: Social engineering attacks & Security vulnerabilities
                         ▪   Social Engineering Attacks: Phishing, Vishing, Smishing
▪  Leakware Attacks:   Also known as exfiltration attacks and combine a standard attack, which encrypts data, with the theft of data. 

Immediately enact a short, testable prevention checklist: apply critical patches, enable phishing‑resistant multi‑factor authentication (MFA), verify offline backups, and ensure DDoS mitigation; run a tabletop drill to validate roles and recovery steps.
```

```
▪   Threat landscape is dynamic: it describes the current set of threats an organization faces, shaped by technology changes (new software, devices), work patterns (remote/hybrid), and emerging malware trends. Regular reassessment keeps defenses aligned with evolving risks.
▪   Attack vectors and entry points are distinct but related: an attack vector is the method a criminal uses (for example, phishing or malware), while entry points are the specific places attackers exploit (email, wireless, remote-access services, or insiders). Focusing on both reduces successful breaches.
▪   Ransomware types and business models: locker ransomware blocks access to a device or service, while crypto ransomware encrypts files. Ransomware-as-a-service (RaaS) is a subscription model that lowers the technical bar for attackers and increases attack volume. Define RaaS when first used: RaaS — ransomware offered as a subscription-like service.
▪   The cybercriminal ecosystem has specialized roles: Initial Access Brokers break into systems and then sell access on underground markets, enabling other attackers to deploy ransomware without advanced skills. This separation increases speed and scale of attacks and demands faster detection of early intrusions.
▪   People are the softest target: social engineering and phishing remain primary vectors because human error (clicking links, exposing credentials, lost devices) often creates the initial foothold. Technical controls must be paired with user training and clear policies.
```
